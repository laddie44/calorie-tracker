# TextCalio Beta Feedback Form

*Reference doc for building the form in Google Forms, Typeform, or Tally.*

---

## Recommended form title

**"Quick feedback on TextCalio — takes 3 minutes"**

## Form description

> Hey! You're one of the first people to try TextCalio and your honest feedback genuinely shapes what we build next.
>
> No right or wrong answers here — if something was confusing or didn't work, that's exactly what I need to know. Takes 3 minutes.

## Questions

---

### Q1 — Did you actually use TextCalio during the beta?

**Type:** Multiple choice (single select)

*This filters everything that follows. Users who didn't engage still tell you something useful.*

- Yes, I used it regularly
- I tried it a few times
- I signed up but barely used it
- I signed up but never tried it

**Type:** Checkbox (select all that apply)

*Shows feature adoption at a glance.*

- Checked my stats ("stats")
- Looked at my dashboard
- Checked my targets ("my targets")
- Logged my weight

**Type:** Multiple choice (single select)

- Always accurate

- Hit or miss
- Often off
- I didn't log any meals by text

**Type:** Rating scale (1–5)

## How to interpret the results

### Step 1 — Segment by Q1 first

Split responses into two groups before reading anything else:
- **Used it** (Q1 = "yes" or "a few times")
- **Didn't use it** (Q1 = "barely" or "never")

Non-users skew every other answer. Read them separately. Their Q5 and Q9 answers often reveal onboarding and activation failures that actual users never surface.

### Step 2 — Read Q5, Q9, Q10 before the ratings

The short-answer questions contain more signal than the multiple-choice ratings. Read them first, before you look at any scores, to avoid anchoring on a number and then rationalizing the qualitative feedback around it.

### Step 3 — Weight Q7 (WTP) heavily

For a beta cohort of friends and family, most people are biased toward being nice. Willingness to pay is harder to fake. "Probably not" from a friend is actually a strong signal.

### Step 4 — Collect Q11 verbatims in a separate list

Every response to Q11 is a positioning sentence candidate. Paste them all into a list. The phrases that appear more than once across different people are your copy.

---

## The 5 most important signals to look for

### Signal 1 — Activation gap (Q1 + Q2)

What % of beta users actually texted a meal? If fewer than half of respondents selected any option in Q2 beyond "None of the above," the product is failing to convert signups into active users. This is an onboarding and first-impression problem, not a product problem.

**Action threshold:** If >40% of respondents never logged a meal, fix onboarding before public launch.

### Signal 2 — Trust in accuracy (Q3)

"Hit or miss" or "often off" responses in Q3 are the single most dangerous signal for TextCalio. The entire value proposition depends on users trusting the numbers. Inaccurate estimates break the product's core promise.

**Action threshold:** If more than 25% of users who logged meals chose "hit or miss" or "often off," prioritize accuracy improvements before scaling.

### Signal 3 — Willingness to pay (Q7)

Friends and family are the warmest possible audience. If even this group says "probably not" or "no," there is a pricing or value perception problem that cold traffic will make much worse. "Probably" and "on the fence" responses here should be treated as soft no's.

**Action threshold:** If fewer than 40% of respondents who used the product say "yes" or "probably," the value is not landing.

### Signal 4 — Retention blockers (Q9 + Q10)

These two questions surface the specific friction or missing features stopping users from committing. Cluster the responses by theme. The themes that appear 3+ times independently are real product gaps. Single responses are probably personal edge cases.

**Common patterns to watch for:** Missing food items, no reminder, not sure what to text, results don't feel personalized, can't edit old logs easily.

### Signal 5 — Competitor framing (Q8)

"About the same" is a red flag. The product needs to feel meaningfully faster and easier than app-based alternatives — not equivalent. If users say it's about the same as MyFitnessPal, the "10 seconds vs 3 minutes" differentiation is not landing in the actual experience.

**Action threshold:** If more than 30% of users who have tried other apps say "about the same" or "worse," the product differentiator is not felt during use.

## Red flags that mean the product/onboarding needs work before public launch

These patterns in the responses indicate problems that word-of-mouth or paid traffic will amplify, not fix.

| Red flag | What it means |
|----------|--------------|
| >40% of respondents never texted a meal | Activation is broken — people sign up and disappear |
| "The welcome SMS was confusing" appears in Q5 | First impression is the problem, not product quality |
| Multiple people mention they forgot to use it | Habit loop is not forming — no reminder, no pull-back mechanism |
| "I didn't know I could send photos" in Q10 | Feature discovery is broken — key differentiator is invisible |
| Q3 skews toward "hit or miss" | Trust in accuracy is the core product promise; this needs fixing first |
| WTP < 40% among active users | Value is not being felt, even by warm early users |
| Q8 skews toward "about the same" | Differentiation is not felt in the actual experience |
| "I forgot the number" or "I lost the link" in Q5/Q12 | Friction in returning to the product — save-Calio-number flow needs work |
| Multiple Q9 responses mentioning reminders | The daily summary is either not being received or not enough |
